# Vowel Harmony and Consonant Assimilation

<attn>All suffixes with vowel harmony or consonant assimilation will be written in capitals, ex. `-lAr` exhibits `a/…ô` harmony.</attn>

There are two main attributes of vowels that are important for Azeri: front vs. back and rounded vs. unrounded. Endings require one or both of these distinctions to match, depending on the vowel in the suffix, which must match the final vowel in a word.

&nbsp; | rounded | unrounded
-------|---------|-----------
front  |  √º √∂    | i e …ô
back   |  u o    | ƒ± a

Vowel wise, there are two main distinctions in endings:

* `a` vs. `…ô` based on front/back
* `i` vs `ƒ±` vs `u` vs `√º` based on front/back, rounded/unrounded

Thus `q…ôl…ôm:pen` ‚Üí `q…ôl…ôml…ôr:pens` or `kitab:book` ‚Üí `kitablar:books`. 

Consnant assimilation happens in two capacities:

1. As part of a suffix
2. Depending on the final consonant of a word and the first consonant of a suffix

**Part of a suffix**

Some suffix's final consonant will change based on the front/back-ness of the vowel. Thus if it is a front vowel, the consonant will be `k` and if back, the consonant will be `q`. For example, with `Biz s√ºr√ºc√ºyIQ:We are drivers` ‚Üí `Biz suÃàruÃàc√ºyuÃàk`, first the `I` turns to `√º` because the last vowel `√º` is front and rounded. Secondly, the `Q` turns to `k` because `√º` is a front vowel.

**Based on final consonant**

If a suffix which begins with a vowel is added to a word that ends in `q` or `k`, that final consonant changes to `ƒü` and `y`, respectively. For example, `usÃßaq-ƒ±n-dƒ±r` ‚Üí `usÃßagÃÜƒ±ndƒ±r:is the child's`.

Note that _k_ may not change to _y_ if it is the end of the root of a verb. For example, in _M…ônd…ôn …ôl √ß…ôk…ô bil…ôrs…ôn!_, we see it is _√ß…ôk…ô_, not _√ß…ôy…ô_, as one might expect.

**Based on final consonant of a verbal root**

If a verbal root ends in `t` and is followed by a suffix that begins with a vowel, the final consonant will change to `d`. Thus, `gedir…ôm:I'm going` (rather than `getir…ôm`). There are some exceptions to this, see EA, pg 86.

## Relative Suffix (-kI)

<attn>`-kI` can only be attached to nouns in LOC or GEN.<cite>[EoA, 105](#eoa)</cite></attn>

To form relative clauses, add `-kI` to the last word in the clause. For example, <ex>_Yazƒ±cÃßƒ± kitabxanadakƒ± kitablarƒ± yazdƒ±_ ‚ß∏ The author wrote the books which are in the library</ex>. `Kitabxanada:in the library` is the clause we want to relativize. Thus, adding `-kƒ±` makes it "which are in the library" and putting it in front of the `kitablarƒ±:books.ACC` makes the relative phrase modify the word books. When attached to nouns in GEN, it functions much like the English phrasing "that which is".

### Future-in-the-Past (FUTPST)

Azeri has a tense that is referred to on Wikipedia as "future-in-the-past". It is used to talk about things that would have happened had the situation in the past been different. "Studied" would be the past conditional ("if you had") and "would be" is the "future-in-the-past", since it is the speculative future result of a past action.

As with the standard future, the future-in-the-past is divided into definite and indefinite forms, which are formed by adding `-dI` to the future forms.

## im…ôk (to be)

The only irregular verb in Azeri. Exists only as a suffix and is attached to the final word of a sentence in PRS. For example, <ex>_M…ôn professoram_ ‚ß∏ I am a professor.</ex> `im…ôk` is only used in PRS or PST. `olmaq`, a regular verb, is used in all other tenses.

As with all suffixes in Azeri, if a suffix is attached to a word ending in a vowel, a "buffer consonant"<cite>[EoA, 21](#eoa)</cite> `y` is added. Thus, `suÃàruÃàc√º:driver` ‚Üí `M…ôn suÃàruÃàc√ºy…ôm:I am a driver`.

## Serial Verbs

In most cases, the subsequent verbs in a series of verbs (ex. to see in "I want to go to the movie") are placed before the declined verb. Thus, <ex>_M…ôn kinoya g…ôtm…ôk ist…ôyir…ôm_ ‚ß∏ I want to go to the movies</ex>.

As with all rules, there are exceptions. Two common verbs, `sevm…ôk:to love` and `xosÃßlamaq:to like`, require the subsequent verbs, still in the infinitive, to be marked as if a direct object. That is, keep `-mAQ` and add `-I`. For example, <ex>_M…ôn oxumagÃÜƒ± sevir…ôm_ ‚ß∏ I like reading</ex>. ## Verbal Suffixes

These suffixes, when appended to the root of the verb, change the meaning from the base meaning of the root. In some cases, additional meanings have become associated with the suffixed form. ### Passive Voice

There are 5 ways to form the passive voice, based on the final letter of the verbal stem (i.e. without `-mAQ`).

If the stem ends in...

# Sentence Structures

## Contrast (is…ô & b…ôs)

When contrasting two clauses (similar to English's _but_), `is…ô` is used. ƒ∞s…ô "is the frozen conditional form of the auxiliary verb `i-` (to be)".<cite>[EA, 15](#ea)</cite> For example, <ex>_M…ôn m√º…ôllim…ôm, s…ôn is…ô t…ôl…ôb…ôs…ôn_ ‚ß∏ I am a teacher, but you are a student.</ex> It occurs after the subject in the second clause. Contrast, if you will, with `b…ôs`, which contrasts between speakers, often after a question, such as in the common `b…ôs s…ôn?:and you?`.

## There is... (var)

The verb `var` is placed at the end of the sentence, to indicate "there is/are...". To express the past tense (e.g. there were), var is paired `idi:was`.

## Expressing Need (lazƒ±m)

Need or necessity is expressed with the adjective _lazƒ±m_. The one needing something is in DAT and the thing needed is in NOM. Since it is an adjective, the verb for the sentence is "to be".

## Expressing Ability (-A bilm…ôk, olar/olmas)

Expressing the ability to do something can be done in two diffrent ways.

The first is done with a special form of the verb. It is formed by removing the infinitive ending and appending `-(y)A`. This special form is paired with `bilm…ôk:to know`. For example, <ex>_M…ôn g√∂r…ô bilir…ôm_ ‚ß∏ I am able to see</ex>.

The second is with _olar_ for positive statements and _olmas_ for negative statements. The verb is left in the infinitive.

## Relative Conjunction (ki)

Similar to English's conjunction "that", _ki_ conjoins two sentences. It is required and is always followed by a comma. For example, <ex>_M…ôn s…ôn…ô dedim ki, onlar evd…ô deyil_ ‚ß∏ I told you that they are not home</ex>.

**daha**

Indicates the comparative degree of an adjective, thus, `daha b√∂y√ºk:bigger`.

**…ôn**

**Ablative case**

## Possession

<attn>The possessive suffix (‚ë¢ or ‚ë£) occurs before any case ending. In the 3rd person, when this occurs, a buffer consonant _n_ is always added.</attn>

Possession can be expressed in multiple ways.

‚ë† LOC + `var`
: The possessor takes LOC and the verb is `var`. This type of possession emphasizes the possessor<cite>[EoA, 47](#eoa)</cite> and is often temporary<cite>[EA, 80](#ea)</cite> or transitory (e.g. a mailcarrier with mail).

‚ë° GEN + to be 
: The possessor takes GEN and the verb is to be. The possessed object precedes the possessor.

‚ë¢ GEN + POSS
: The possessor takes GEN and the possessed item takes the POSS case. If there are more than one noun doing the possessing, the last POSS marking agrees with the last noun. This is the most common form of indicating possession.<cite>[EoA, 78](#eoa)</cite>

The ‚ë¢ form has these POSS suffixes:

&nbsp; | SG | pl
-|-----|-----
1|-(I)m|-(I)mIz
2|-(I)n|-(I)nIz
3|-(s)I|-(s)I

Since the only the 3rd person endings are ambiguous, a pronoun is only required for 3SG and 3PL. Otherwise, the GEN pronoun can be dropped.<cite>[EoA, p53]</cite>

For chained possession, ex. my friend's cat's encyclopedia, each object possessed must get a POSS suffix and each object possessing must get the GEN suffix. This means that some objects will have multiple suffixes. For example, <ex>_Universitet-in boÃàyuÃàk kitabxana-sƒ±-n-ƒ±n t…ôz…ô kitablar-ƒ±_ ‚ß∏ The university's big libray's new books</ex>. How does this work?

1. First we have "university's big library", which is `universitet-in boÃàyuÃàk kitabxana-sƒ±`.
2. Then we have the "library's new books", which would be `kitabxana-nƒ±n t…ôz…ô kitablar-ƒ±`.
3. We put it all together, going left to right, meaning that before the library possesses anything, it is also possessed, so the GEN suffix attaches to the POSS suffix as seen above. Voila!

‚ë£ POSS + `var`
: Similar to ‚ë†, this emphasizes the object possessed.<cite>[EoA, 79](#eoa)</cite> In colloquial speech, in 1st or 2nd person, one may hear the POSS marker on `var`, rather than on the object. For example, <ex>_Varƒ±mdƒ±r_ ‚ß∏ I have it</ex> or <ex>_Yoxumdur_ ‚ß∏ I don't have it</ex>.<cite>[EA, 81](#ea)</cite>

‚ë§ POSS
: Just POSS is used in compound nouns, such as "radiator hat". The first part of the compound noun is unmarked and the second part is marked with POSS, e.g. `konsert bileti:concert ticket`. This becomes the noun itself, so case markings will append to the POSS marked noun (buffered with `-n-` if 3SG POSS), ex. `m…ôkt…ôb kitablarƒ±nda:in the school books`.

--EX--

## Negation

<attn>Note that Azeri exhibits double negation, thus _he√ß n…ô g√∂rm√ºr…ôm:I see nothing_.</attn>

There are three different ways of negating a sentence. 

**To Be sentences (deyil)**

When something _is not_ something, the word `deyil` is used. In spoken Azeri, it is often pronounced [d√∂:l].<cite>[EA, 20](#ea)</cite> The personal suffix is affixed to `deyil`.

**There is... (yoxdur / yox)**

`var` from existential sentences is replaced with `yoxdur` in the negative. In the past tense, it is `yox idi`.

Colloquially, you may hear _yox_ used with personal endings. For example, <ex>_M…ônd…ô feys yoxdur_ ‚ß∏ I am not on Facebook</ex> versus <ex>_M…ôn feysd…ô yoxam_ ‚ß∏ I am not on Facebook</ex>. Compare this with _M…ônd…ô deyil…ôm_. This sentence would mean something more like "I am not presently on Facebook (i.e. logged in)" versus using _yoxdur_ which implies that you don't have a Facebook account at all.

**Regular Verbs (-mA)**

The suffix -mA (or -m if `r` is in the following tense suffix, such as in the present tense) is inserted between the verbal root and the tense suffix. Thus, `M…ôn bilir…ôm:I know` ‚Üí `M…ôn bilmir…ôm:I don't know`.

In IMP, where one would be tempted to look for a word meaning "don't", as in "don't do this", the negative marker suffices.

--EX--

--ENDEX--

## The three uses of da/d…ô

`dA` can be used in a variety of ways, even all three within one sentence. The vowel in `dA` changes based on the immediately preceding vowel.

1. Adds emphasis when following a verb. It's often to heighten the emotion of the sentence, such as emphasizing "on" in "come on".
2. Equivalent to "also", following the word which is "also" had
3. A conjunction, "even though" or "despite the fact". The clause preceding `dA` is the "even though X" clause and is either in the past or non-past conditional tense.

--EX--

M…ônim d…ô ail…ôm var.

--ENDEX--

## Emphatic Expressions

In music or poetry, and occasionally in spoken language, emphatic "I/you/we/etc. are" statements can be made by attaching the copula to the personal pronoun, followed by the adjective. ## Direct and Indirect Speech (dem…ôk ki)

Direct speech is offset with quotes and followed by a conjugated form of `dem…ôk:to say`. Thus, <ex>_M…ôn ona "saƒüa d√∂n" dedim_ ‚ß∏ I said to him "turn to the right".</ex>

# Adverbs

Adverbs can often be formed by dropping the infinitive marker (`-mAQ`), adding `-A` and duplicating the word. For instance, `baxa-baxa:watchingly` (from `baxmaq:to watch`) or `g√ºl…ô-g√ºl…ô:laughingly` (from `g√ºlm…ôk:to laugh`).

In other cases, it may be a noun followed by `s…ôkild…ô:in the image`. This indicates perhaps more literally "in the image of X", but is used much as English adds `-ly` to the ends of adjectives. For example, `h…ôv…ôskar:amature` ‚Üí `h…ôv…ôskar ≈ü…ôkild…ô:amaturishly`.

# Participles

Participles are often equivalent to English relative clauses (which/who does X).
